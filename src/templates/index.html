Will Luka Doncic win NBA Rookie of the Year?

By John Hancock | Editor, Data and Interactives

Published Month Date, Year

    When it comes to Luka Doncic, there was never a doubt for the Dallas Mavericks.

    The team’s front office did years of research and scouting on the Slovenian teenager before shipping Trae Young and a protected first round pick in next year’s draft to Atlanta for Doncic in a draft-night trade. Team owner Mark Cuban said getting Doncic in the draft was a "no-brainer," and ESPN analyst Mike Schmidt said Doncic was "the best basketball player in the draft."

    Doncic has backed up the hype on the court. The 19-year-old phenom has planted his flag as the Mavericks go-to option, leading the team in scoring and usage percentage.

    But is Doncic the NBA Rookie of the Year? A Dallas Morning News analysis used 10 different metrics to rate this season's rookie class, assigning each player two scores based on traditional and advanced statistics. Read more about how those scores are determined below.

    The result is clear. Doncic is the favorite, followed closely by Phoenix Suns big man Deandre Ayton, the first pick in last June’s draft. See how Doncic gains an edge over Ayton and how he stacks up against the rest of the class below.

    Putting up the numbers

    Doncic has no trouble filling up a box score. He leads this rookie class in scoring at points per game and ranks in the top three in per-game averages for rebounds (), assists () and steals ()

    Those numbers are impressive when compared to his fellow rookies, but historically, they are off the charts. In the history of the NBA, only five other rookies have averaged 19 points, five rebounds and five assists per game: Oscar Robertson, Michael Jordan, LeBron James, Tyreke Evans and Grant Hill. That’s rarified company to be in.

    Beyond the traditional stats, Doncic’s performance across a range of advanced NBA metrics puts him at the front of this year’s class as well, just ahead of Ayton. But while Doncic and Ayton both rank in the top three in winshares, box plus/minus and player efficiency, Doncic holds a significant edge in one other key metric: usage percentage.

    Doncic leads the class in usage percentage at %, and is one of only two rookies that currently qualify for the league’s points per game leaderboard and have a usage percentage above 27%. Ayton, by comparison, ranks ninth in the class at %.

    While Doncic and Ayton are near equals in how often they are on the court for their respective teams, the Mavericks are relying on Doncic to do more in the offense, and Doncic’s efficiency hasn’t suffered from the additional workload.

    Recent history is on his side

    If Doncic finishes the season where he is now, leading all rookies in our traditional and advanced metric scores, he’ll be the seventh rookie since 2008-09 to do so. The other six all won NBA Rookie of the Year. Three of the remaining four rookie of the year winners finished first in at least one of our metric scores.

    Currently, Doncic leads Ayton by in our traditional metrics score and in our advanced metrics score.

    In the past 10 years, only Andrew Wiggins took home the best rookie award while not finishing first in either metric. The Timberwolves forward set himself apart in points per game, though. Wiggins averaged 5.0 points more than the Los Angeles Lakers’ Jordan Clarkson and 6.7 more than rookie of the year runner-up Nikola Mirotic.

    Doncic, who leads the rookie class in scoring, is averaging more points per game than Ayton, the next highest scoring rookie.

    Best in a decade?

    As previously stated, Doncic is having a statistical rookie campaign for the ages, placing him in some elite company when it comes to points, rebounds and assists.

    Given that, it’s no surprise that when Doncic is compared to the other rookies in the last 10 years, he’s setting himself apart by having a season similar to the rookie campaigns of Damian Lillard, Tyreke Evans and Kyrie Irving in terms of our two scoring systems.

    And it’s not out of the realm of possibility that Doncic matches rookie seasons by Blake Griffin, Karl-Anthony Towns and Ben Simmons by the end of the year. Since Doncic doesn’t have a full season’s worth of games yet, the potential number of winshares available to him is deflated. Given Doncic’s level of play thus far, it’s not inconceivable that his final total in winshares gives him a boost, placing him among that upper echelon.

    As it is, Doncic ranks sixth out of the rookies since 2008-09 in our analysis’ advanced metrics score and eighth in our traditional metrics score. When it comes to points per game alone, Doncic’s is fourth behind Blake Griffin, Donovan Mitchell and Tyreke Evans.

    What about Ayton?

    Doncic’s successes on the court and the hype surrounding his rookie campaign may lead to him running away with the rookie of the year voting. But by a pure numbers standpoint, the Suns’ Ayton is a legitimate threat.

    Ayton’s dominance on the glass ( rebounds per game) and scoring output ( points per game, second only to Doncic), make him a legitimate contender for the award. And his advanced metrics, outside of usage percentage are all comparable to Doncic’s.

    But what Doncic has going for him that Ayton doesn’t is screen time. Doncic is the Mavericks’ first option on offense. In Phoenix, Devin Booker is the Suns best player, and Ayton even falls behind T.J. Warren in scoring amongst his team.

    And then there’s the hype and clutch play. The 19-year-old Mavericks rookie has made plenty of highlight reels, including his 11-0 scoring run over the final 2 minutes of play to lift Dallas to a victory over in-state rival Houston in December. That’s just one instance of the 10 times Doncic has hit a field goal to tie or take the lead in a game in the last two minutes of the fourth quarter or overtime, the most in the NBA.

    So, while the rookie seasons of Doncic and Ayton are comparable within our metrics, Doncic still holds the edge in the numbers and passes the eyeball test, almost surely guaranteeing that he takes home the rookie of the year hardware.

    How we scored the rookies

    Each player in this season’s rookie class was rated against one another in ten statistical categories. Our traditional score uses per game numbers for points, rebounds, assists, steals and blocks. The advanced score is comprised of minutes per game, winshares, box plus/minus, player efficiency rating and usage percentage.

    While minutes per game is traditional statistic, it's included with the advanced metrics intentionally. Usage percentage measures only the percentage of plays used by a player while he is on the floor. The more a player is on the floor, the more impact his usage percentage has, and typically, player‘s efficiency decreases as usage increases. Players that can maintain efficiency at high usage rates over increased playing time are more valuable to their team.

    To generate a player's total score, players were assigned a z-score for each stat. The z-score measures not just where a player ranks among a group, but how much better — or worse — that player is versus the group’s average in that statistic. Those scores are combined to calculate our total metric scores. In short, the better each player is than the average player in a statistic, the higher their total score.

    Winshares (WS)

    Winshares are an estimate of the number of wins a player has contributed to his team. Source: Basketball Reference

    Box plus/minus (BPM)

    Box plus/minus is a box-score based metric for evaluating a player’s contribution to his team relative to the league average per 100 possessions. Source: Basketball Reference

    Usage percentage (USG)

    Usage percentage is an estimate of the percentage of team plays used by a player while he is on the floor. Any play that ends in a player’s field goal attempt, free throw attempt or turnover is considerd to be used by that player. Source: Basketball Reference

    Player efficiency rating (PER)

    Player efficiency rating is a per-minute rating of the sum of a player’s positive accomplishments (field goals, assists, rebounds, etc.) minus their negative accomplishments (personal fouls, turnovers, missed field goals, etc.). Source: Basketball Reference
